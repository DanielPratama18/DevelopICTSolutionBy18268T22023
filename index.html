Cybersecurity

Referred to as information security, cybersecurity refers to the practice of ensuring the integrity, confidentiality, and availability (ICA) of information. Cybersecurity is comprised of an evolving set of tools, risk management approaches, technologies, training, and best practices designed to protect networks, devices, programs, and data from attacks or unauthorized access.



Why is cybersecurity important


  • protection: Safeguards sensitive information from theft, damage, or unauthorized access, ensuring privacy and preventing identity theft or fraud.

  • Business continuity: Ensures availability and integrity of IT systems, allowing organizations to maintain normal operations and minimize downtime.

  • Reputation and trust: Helps maintain an organization's reputation by demonstrating a commitment to data protection and privacy, fostering trust among customers and partners.

  • Legal and regulatory compliance: Ensures businesses adhere to industry-specific regulations and avoid fines, legal actions, or potential loss of business.

  • Economic impact: Protects businesses and economies from lost revenue, reduced consumer confidence, and increased costs associated with recovery and remediation after a cyberattack.

  • National security: Guards critical infrastructure against cyberattacks that could result in physical damage, public safety risks, or geopolitical tensions.
  • Supporting innovation: Enables digital transformation and technological advancements by ensuring new technologies are secure and reliable.

Common types of cybersecurity


1. Network security: Protects the integrity and usability of network infrastructure by defending against unauthorized access, network-based attacks, and data breaches.
2. Application security: Focuses on safeguarding applications from vulnerabilities, threats, and attacks by implementing security measures during the development process and regularly updating and patching applications.
3. Endpoint security: Secures endpoints, such as desktops, laptops, and mobile devices, from potential threats by deploying antivirus software, firewalls, and other security solutions.
4. Data security: Ensures the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of data, both in transit and at rest, through encryption, access controls, and backup and recovery strategies.
5. Identity and access management (IAM): Involves the management of user identities and controlling their access to resources, employing authentication and authorization mechanisms, such as single sign-on (SSO) and multi-factor authentication (MFA).
6. Cloud security: Addresses the security challenges and risks associated with cloud computing, including data privacy, compliance, and access management.
7. Incident response: Aims to minimize the impact of security incidents by establishing a plan to detect, respond to, and recover from breaches, attacks, or other security events.
8. Disaster recovery and business continuity: Develops strategies to recover IT systems and maintain business operations in the event of a cyberattack, natural disaster, or other disruptive events.
9. Security awareness and training: Enhances the security posture of an organization by providing employees with the knowledge and skills needed to identify and respond to potential threats, such as phishing attacks or social engineering.
10. Physical security: Protects the physical assets of an organization, such as buildings, computer systems, and equipment, from theft, damage, or unauthorized access.

Data Breach For ICT Solution

A data breach is a security incident in which information is accessed without authorization. Data breaches can hurt businesses and consumers in a variety of ways. They are a costly expense that can damage lives and reputations and take time to repair.

As technology progresses, more and more of our information has been moving to the digital world. As a result, cyberattacks have become increasingly common and costly. We’ve created a 7-step checklist to use in the case of a data breach. Depending on the severity of the breach and the information exposed, you’ll need to apply some or all of these steps to varying degree. Seven Step Checklist

  • Confirm the breach if your information was exposed
  • Find out what type of data was stolen
  • Accept the breached company's offer(s) to help
  • strengthen your online logins, passwords and security
  • Contact the right people and take additional action

Confirm the breach and if your information was exposed

1. Confirm the breach and if your information was exposed. The first step is to confirm that a breach actually occurred. When a data breach occurs, scammers may reach out to you posing as the breached company to try to obtain more of your personal information. Don't fall for fake emails. Go to the company's secure website and/or call the company to confirm the breach and whether your information was involved.

Find out what type of data was stolen

2. Find out what type of data was stolen. Why does the type of information exposed matter? While stolen credit cards and the like can be cancelled and replaced, it's quite difficult to obtain a new Social Security number. And fraudsters can do a lot more with your SSN and other unique, sensitive PII than they can accomplish with an email or credit card account.

Accept the breached company's offer(s) to help

3. Accept the breached company's offer(s) to help. If a breached company offers assistance after a security incident, it can be beneficial to accept their help, depending on the specifics of the situation.

Change Password and Security Q&A

4. Change Password and Security Q&A. Changing your password and security questions and answers (Q&A) is an essential step to protect your online accounts and personal information.

Contact the right people to tack action

5. Contact the right people to tack action. This is where the type of data stolen really comes into play. If your credit and/or debit card information was stolen, you should reach out immediately to the financial institution(s) to cancel your card and request a new one. However, if personal information like your Social Security number was stolen, it may be easier for you to become a victim of identity theft.
