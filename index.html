  IDENTIFYING CRITICAL SYSTEM

Good System Need

  • It should reliably back up all essential data, applications, and configurations, ensuring nothing critical is missed.
  • It should be capable of quickly and fully restoring systems and data after a disruption or disaster.
  • The backup data should be securely stored and encrypted to prevent unauthorized access.
  • The system should include capabilities for regular testing to ensure that recovery procedures work as intended.
  • The system should scale effectively as the volume of data and the infrastructure of the organization grow.

If System Bad

  • If the system fails to backup data reliably, it could result in irreversible data loss.
  • If recovery capabilities are inadequate, it could lead to extended downtime, impacting business continuity.
  • If the backup storage is not secure, it could lead to data breaches.
  • If not tested regularly, the system might fail during an actual disaster recovery situation.
  • If the system cannot scale with the organization, it may become insufficient over time.

System Need Support

  • The system needs regular checks and updates to ensure it is functioning correctly and optimally.
  • As the organization evolves, backup and recovery policies should be updated to reflect changes in data and infrastructure.
  • The system needs continuous security support to protect against data breaches or loss.
  • Regular disaster recovery drills and tests are necessary to ensure the system is ready when needed.
  • IT staff and key personnel should receive training on how to use the system and perform recovery procedures.

Who will user System

  • IT Team: They would manage and maintain the backup and disaster recovery system.
  • Security Team: They would monitor the system and address any security concerns.
  • Operations Team: They might use the system to retrieve data when needed and would be involved in disaster recovery procedures.
  • Management: They would use reports and insights from the system to make informed decisions regarding business continuity planning.
  • All Employees: Depending on the organization's size and structure, all employees might interact with the system to some degree, such as restoring accidentally deleted files or accessing restored systems after a disaster.

Task One

What factors would need to be considered in determining whether this new system will be critical to the business and what the impact might be if it fails?

Category Description
Good Impact

A new system could be critical to your business if it significantly improves efficiency, profitability, or provides a competitive advantage. Consider the following factors:

  • Time savings: If the system can automate certain tasks or processes, thus freeing up employee time for other tasks.

  • Cost savings: If the system can save money, either through automation or by reducing the need for certain resources.

  • Competitive advantage: If the system gives your business an edge over your competition, for example, by allowing you to provide a unique service.

  • Compliance and risk management: If the system helps your company to comply with regulations and manage risk, particularly in privacy and contingency planning.

    		•
    Bad Side

    The potential negative impact if the system fails could be significant, depending on the system's importance. Here are some factors to consider:

  • Downtime: If the system fails, how long would it take to get it back up and running? What would the impact be on your business during this time?

  • Data loss: If the system is responsible for storing or managing data, what would the impact of a data loss be? Could it lead to regulatory fines, or damage your company's reputation?

  • Resource drain: If the system is complex and requires a lot of maintenance, it could consume resources that could be better used elsewhere.

  • Security: If the system has vulnerabilities, it could expose your business to cyber threats.

    		•
    What We Need to Know

    Need to gather a significant amount of information to properly evaluate the potential impact of the new system:

  • Functionality: Functionality: What tasks or processes will the system manage?

  • Criticality: How critical are these tasks to your business operations?

  • Dependency: How dependent are other tasks or processes on the ones managed by the system?

  • Data: What kind of data will the system handle? Is it sensitive or regulated data?

  • Resilience: How robust is the system? What kind of fail safes or redundancies does it have?

  • Support: What support will be available if something goes wrong?

    		•
    We Need a Good System

    In order for the system to be beneficial and worth the potential risks, it needs to be a good system. This means:

  • It is reliable and doesn't fail often.

  • It is secure and has strong measures in place to protect data.

  • It is user-friendly and doesn't require extensive training to use.

  • It is flexible and can adapt as your business grows or changes.

  • It offers good value for money in terms of its cost versus the benefits it provides.

    		•

    In conclusion, a proper assessment and analysis should be conducted before implementing the system to understand how it will affect a business. The business should also have a contingency plan in place to deal with any potential failures or issues.
